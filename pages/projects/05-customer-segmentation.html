Customer Segmentation with Artificial Ecommerce Data

LIVE 2024.02
PySpark SQL Machine Learning RFM Analysis
Overview

A collaborative project with fellow students from the Columbia Data Analytics Bootcamp. Using e-commerce data from Kaggle, the goal was to uncover trends in business performance, analyze customer behaviors, and leverage machine learning to predict churn and identify low-value customers. My primary role covered ETL, feature engineering, and the machine learning components.

/// Github Repository — Customer Segmentation
Methods

Approach

ETL and Feature Engineering

Given the scale of the dataset (250,000 records), ETL and feature engineering were handled with PySpark and SQL. The primary table was split into three: a customer profile table aggregated by customerID, a sales-by-date table aggregated on purchase date, and a transformed table with encoded categorical features prepared for ML.

Exploratory Data Analysis

EDA examined overall business performance. Revenue trajectory showed expected growth. Books and clothing category sales dominated home and electronics, with no notable gender-based differences in shopping category. Sales count, order totals, and product price were normally distributed, indicating a healthy baseline.

RFM Analysis

RFM (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) analysis was used to segment customers based on buying behavior. Scores from 1–5 were assigned across all three dimensions (with recency inverted, as a smaller date gap is more favorable). Customers were binned into low, mid, and high value segments. Most customers fell into the low-value bin. Overlapping RFM value segments with customer segments showed that recency of purchase was the dominant driver of RFM score — more so than frequency or monetary value.

Predicting Churn and Customer Value

RandomForestClassifier, optimized with Sequential Backward Selection and GridSearchCV, was used to classify low-value customers and predict churn from initial purchase data. F1 score was used for the low-value model due to class imbalance; accuracy was used for the churn model.

In the low-value customer model, purchase date scored over 20% in feature importance, suggesting that external factors — economic, social, or political — play a significant role in this segment, alongside price and total purchase amount. In the churn model, purchase amount, date, and price contributed roughly equally, with no single feature dominating.

While both models show relative robustness, precision scores were poor in both cases due to class imbalance. These models would not be recommended for deployment as-is given the high false-positive rate. However, the F1 and accuracy scores confirm that predicting churn and customer value from initial purchase data is achievable with additional feature engineering and data collection.
